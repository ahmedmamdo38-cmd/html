Before 'Pong,' There Was 'Tennis for Two'

Before the era of electronic ping pong, hungry yellow dots, plumbers, mushrooms, and fire-flowers, people waited in line to play video games at roller-skating rinks, arcades, and other hangouts. More than fifty years ago, before either arcades or home video games, visitors waited in line at Brookhaven National Laboratory to play “Tennis for Two,” an electronic tennis game that is unquestionably a forerunner of the modern video game.

Tennis for Two was first introduced on October 18, 1958, at one of the Lab’s annual visitors’ days. Two people played the electronic tennis game with separate controllers that connected to an analog computer and used an oscilloscope for a screen. The game’s creator, William Higinbotham, was a nuclear physicist lobbied for nuclear nonproliferation as the first chair of the Federation of American Scientists.

Gameplay Characteris

Screen & Display

  • Shown on a 5-inch cathode-ray oscilloscope
  • Side view: you see the ball trajectory going over a horizontal "ground" line
  • Only a moving dot and a line — no characters, no net visible on early versions (a net was added in the 1959 update)

Controls

Each player had a handheld controller:

  • One button to strike the ball
  • One rotary knob to choose hitting angle
  • Timing mattered — press too late and you “missed”

Objective

Hit the ball so that it travels over the net and lands within the opponent’s court. If it lands outside or hits the net → that player loses a point.

Under The Hood

Unlike later digital video games, this was driven by analog circuitry:

Component Function
Analog computer Calculated ball motion using real-time physics
oscilloscope Displayed the ball's position as voltages controlling X and Y axes
Custom circuitry Modeled gravity, bounce and air resistance

Real Physics Simulation

The computer calculated:

  • Parabolic trajectoryunder gravity
  • Bounce angles and velocity reduction
  • Spin and wind were not simulated, but the flight looked realistic

This made it surprisingly advanced compared to many later early games

Timeline and Public Reaction

Year Event
1958 First version displayed huge success with visitors
1959 Upgraded version: larger screen, added net, better visuals
1960s Dismantled, parts reused for lab work
1980s Recognized in video game history as a major milestone
1997 Game rebuilt for historical preservation

Visitors loved it long lines formed to play.Many described it as more fun than anything else at the exhibit.

But… Higinbotham never patented it, even though he easily could have which meant no commercial success or recognition at the time.
